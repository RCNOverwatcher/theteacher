Design validation

Introduction
Whenever we design something, especially where we are designing it for others to use, we need to check carefully that it really is fit for purpose.

We all have our own backgrounds, education, experiences, our own formal and informal language, culture, ethics and way of doing things. Although designers of systems in general and user interfaces in particular always like to think they know best and can design things for others to use, that rarely is the case. 

Design reviews
Designs must always be checked and checked regularly. They can be checked before a product is built in a number of ways.

    • They can be checked by the designer themselves, checking that they have indeed covered all the points in a specification.
    • The can be checked by doing a peer-review. This is where a designer asks other designers not associated with a design to cast a critical eye over it and give feedback.
    • They can be reviewed by the customer, the actual users and any other stakeholders, such as the managers who will look after the system or the technical people who will maintain it. This can be achieved by creating a mock-up of the design or a prototype, as customers may not be technical and may have trouble visualising a design as easily as the designers can.

Prototypes
It is always a good idea to create a prototype before investing a lot of time and energy on the actual final product. It is a quick and easy way to show others what you are planning to do and get feedback. It is a great way to check that you have understood what you have been told. It is a really good way of others spotting quickly that something is not quite right and needs to be modified or that something has been missed out and needs to be included or has been added but isn't necessary. Other people may not have the same expertise as you. By presenting something that looks like the final product, even if it is far from functional, you are including everyone in the review process, whatever their background. Prototypes also help to reassure the customer that they are going to get what they need and are paying for and is a good way of ensuring excellent on-going relationships between the customer and supplier. 

Checking the user interface
One area that needs special consideration is the user interface. Is it fit for purpose? How do you know? Something may look perfect on paper but just doesn't work as well in practice. How can you prove it works well? It is important to check with all relevant stakeholders that the user interface is fit for purpose. Again, prototypes can help here, but ultimately, the final product's interface must be tested. That means sitting down with users, devising a set of tasks and seeing how they get on. It means providing a way of getting feedback from your users and then listening carefully to your users when they spend time giving you feedback, taking action wherever possible and explaining to them when something can't be done about a concern. There are various tests, tasks and questions that can be worked through when sitting down with users and asking them for feedback. For example, 

    • Is the user interface easy to navigate around?
    • Are the menus clear? 
    • Is it intuitive?
    • Is anything frustrating to use?
    • When they get stuck, is it easy to get help?
    • Does the system freeze or crash at any point?
    • Is it easy to use over longer periods?
    • Is the jargon easy to understand?
    • Could the layout be improved?
    • Are the fonts readable?
    • Can users work quickly?
    • Are there any areas where users have to slow down?
    • Can they think of anything that could be changed or added that might make their lives using the interface a little easier?
