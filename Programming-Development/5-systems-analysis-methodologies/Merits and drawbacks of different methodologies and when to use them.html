Merits and drawbacks of different methodologies and when to use them

The waterfall model pros

    1. You can identify potential problems and investigate them thoroughly during the design phase, rather than the development phase.
    2. The customer knows what they are going to get.
    3. Excellent documentation is produced at each stage, which can be both reassuring to the customer and aid tracking down the source of any problems.
    4. Excellent documentation and solid project management ensure that staff changes don't adversely affect a project.
    5. It's a linear process, so it's easy to understand.

The waterfall model cons

    1. It can be difficult for a customer to know about and specify what they want at the beginning of a project. They may not know or understand what is possible with technology.
    2. It can be difficult to specify what is required at the beginning of a project. If the specifications are wrong from the start, it can badly impact on a project.
    3. It's often difficult to identify problems that might occur in one area as a result of work being done in a different area.
    4. When there is a modification, there is often a long and involved backtracking process required, which can slow down the project and make the project overly complex.
    5. Bugs in one area of code may not be discovered early enough, or their impact on other areas not identified until it's too late - and then costly changes will be required!

Agile methodologies pros

    1. Working software can be produced much more quickly than a waterfall model, and at a known pace.
    2. Much closer collaboration between all stakeholders.
    3. Constant customer feedback helps the project stay on track. There are no nasty surprises that only get revealed at the end of a project.
    4. Changes can easily be incorporated at any time.
    5. You can include any recent developments in technology because it is easy to include such changes.
    6. Highly transparent.
    7. A project can go 'live' (with a few reduced or missing features) even if it is only 80% complete.

Agile methodologies cons

    1. These methodologies may not be as easy to understand as a simple linear model.
    2. Documentation can be neglected because the focus is on producing software.
    3. This is a relatively knew way of working, which may not fit into the experiences of customers.

Agile, RAD and Spiral methodologies
In some respects, RAD and Spiral methodologies are forerunners to Agile methodologies. There are some key differences, though and they should certainly be considered different methodologies. RAD and Spiral methodologies are based on the production of a series of prototypes, which are then used to build the product. Agile methodologies don't encourage the development of prototypes - the project story is broken down at the start into a storyboard and then developed in an agreed order. Changes are expected at any time and planned for in Agile methodologies but this is not the case with the RAD and the Spiral approach. RAD and spiral methodology team members are generally specialists in their own fields whereas Agile methodologies encourage everyone to be generalists, able to do any task that needs doing. Similarly, the comunication that takes place in RAD and spiral methodologies can be compartmentalised compared to the more open nature of the Agile approach - the poor communications that can happen easily in RAD, Spiral and also Waterfall projects are always a major reason why projects go off-track or fail altogether. 

How to select a methodology
Agile methodologies are more flexible than the waterfall approach. This is a useful approach if it is difficult for the customer to specify exactly what they need at the start of the project or if there are likely to be changes. If you have a skilled team of programmers and developers who are adaptable and independent, then you are more likely to benefit from using this methodology to manage a project.

If you can guarantee that the requirements won't change, there is little uncertainty of any other changes in the project, the project is a simple one or the customers involved have only have positive experiences of the Waterfall approach, then a Waterfall methodology still might be worth considering, as might the RAD and Spiral methodologies.
